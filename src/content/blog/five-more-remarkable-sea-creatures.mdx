Our first voyage met the clever octopus, the colossal blue whale, and a few other ocean favourites, and you asked us to set sail again. So here we are, back on the water with a fresh logbook and five more animals that earn the word "remarkable" without a single repeat from the first crossing. The sea is deep and the manifest is long, so let us cast off and meet the next watch.

## The leafy sea dragon

If a seahorse went to a costume party dressed as a piece of seaweed, you would have something close to a leafy sea dragon. Found only in the cool waters off southern Australia, it trails leaf-shaped fronds from its body that serve no purpose but disguise, letting it drift among the kelp looking for all the world like a stray bit of plant. It is a feeble swimmer, propelled by fins so small and clear they are almost invisible, so it relies entirely on its camouflage to stay off the menu. Like its seahorse cousins, the male is the one who tends the eggs, carrying them tucked beneath his tail until they hatch.

## The immortal jellyfish

Most animals have one direction of travel through life, but the immortal jellyfish, properly called *Turritopsis dohrnii*, refused to read that particular rulebook. When this thumbnail-sized creature is injured, starving, or simply old, it can transform its grown cells back into an earlier stage of life, settling onto the seabed and beginning again as a young colony. It is the closest thing the animal kingdom has to a rewind button. In practice plenty still get eaten or fall ill, so they are not truly deathless, but in theory a single individual could cycle through this trick over and over, which is about as remarkable as biology gets.

## The pistol shrimp

For an animal barely the length of a finger joint, the pistol shrimp throws an astonishing punch, and it does it without ever landing a blow. One of its claws is oversized and built like a spring-loaded hammer, and when it snaps shut it does so fast enough to fire a bubble across the water. That bubble collapses with such violence that it briefly flashes with light and reaches temperatures rivalling the surface of the sun, stunning prey from a distance. The accompanying crack is so loud that colonies of these shrimp can muddle submarine sonar, making one of the ocean's smallest hunters one of its noisiest residents.

## The anglerfish

Few animals look quite as much like a sea monster as the deep-sea anglerfish, and few have a better excuse. Living far below the reach of sunlight, the female grows a fleshy lure on her head that glows with light made by colonies of bacteria, dangling it like a tiny lantern to draw curious prey straight into an enormous, toothy mouth. Stranger still is her love life: in some species the much smaller male bites onto her body and slowly fuses to it, sharing her bloodstream and shrinking to little more than a permanent supply of fertiliser for her eggs. It is a grim arrangement by our standards, but a practical one when mates are scarce in the endless dark.

## The giant Pacific octopus

We met the common octopus last time, but its larger relative deserves a stanza of its own. The giant Pacific octopus is the biggest octopus in the world, with an arm span that can stretch wider than a small car and a weight to match, yet it can still pour its boneless body through a gap no larger than a coin. It is every bit as clever as its smaller cousin, known in aquariums for recognising individual keepers, unscrewing jars from the inside, and slipping out of tanks on midnight expeditions. After a life of only a few years it makes one last grand gesture, with the female guarding her eggs without eating until they hatch, then quietly fading away. Even an ending, it seems, can be remarkable.

From a dragon made of leaves to a jellyfish that begins again, the ocean keeps proving it has more wonders than any single voyage can hold. Rest the crew, restock the hold, and keep your curiosity well provisioned, for there are always more remarkable creatures waiting beyond the next swell.
